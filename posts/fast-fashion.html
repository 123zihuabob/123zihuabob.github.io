Documentaries on Fast Fashion: A Reflection on Environmental and Social Impacts

Friday, 29 November 2024

Fast fashion, characterized by rapid production cycles, trend‑driven designs, and affordability, has significantly reshaped consumer behavior and the global economy. While it offers accessible clothing, the industry’s environmental and social costs are undeniable. Americans, for instance, went from buying about 12 garments annually in the 1980s to approximately 68 pieces per year today, illustrating a culture of disposability fostered by fast fashion (Brooks, 2013).

The production process involves multiple stages, each contributing to environmental and social harm. Raw materials like cotton and polyester require vast resources, leading to water pollution and reliance on non‑renewable energy. Textile manufacturing generates substantial waste, as seen in the production of viscose, which discards 70% of harvested wood. Chemical dyes also pollute rivers, particularly in developing countries where regulatory oversight is weak. Manufacturing is labor‑intensive, with most factories in global South nations employing workers under exploitative conditions, driven by low wages and weak labor protections. Finally, overconsumption of fast fashion results in massive landfill waste, worsening environmental degradation (Minhaj, n.d.; DW Documentary, n.d.; Karim, 2014).

The documentaries The Ugly Truth of Fast Fashion and The Clothes We Wear reveal the stark realities of the industry. Both highlight the severe exploitation and environmental destruction caused by global production networks (GPNs). These networks epitomize uneven development by shifting production to low‑cost regions while concentrating profits in developed nations. The reliance on subcontracting and outsourcing enables corporations to sidestep stringent labor and environmental laws. Consequently, countries in the global South bear the brunt of these inequities, with minimal benefit to local communities (Ramamurthy, 2004; Brooks, 2013).

Uneven development within GPNs perpetuates historical inequalities. Wealth and resources flow from poorer regions to wealthier ones, maintaining a system of exploitation. The informal economy and migrant labor play pivotal roles in sustaining this model. Workers, often part of an industrial reserve army, face precarious employment conditions, while consumers in developed nations benefit from affordable clothing. This system not only exacerbates social inequality but also accelerates environmental harm, driven by consumer demand for cheap, disposable goods (Stanes & Gibson, 2017; Corwin, n.d.).

Beyond fast fashion, other industries exhibit similar patterns, such as electronics. Companies like Apple rely on low‑cost production hubs in countries like China and India, leading to resource depletion and environmental degradation. Both industries highlight the environmental and social costs of unchecked globalization and consumerism (Minhaj, n.d.; DW Documentary, n.d.; Corwin, n.d.).

While fast fashion democratizes access to trends, its hidden costs—environmental degradation, labor exploitation, and economic inequality—are unsustainable. Addressing these issues requires robust global regulations and consumer advocacy for ethical practices. By reevaluating production and consumption patterns, the industry can transition toward sustainability and fairness, benefiting both people and the planet.
